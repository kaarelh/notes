


This proposal looks like conditioning predictive models — some issues are discussed in [https://arxiv.org/pdf/2302.00805](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2302.00805) . Dr. Trott posted a previous version of this plan on LW a few days ago ( [https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/Gs7CmLeYgc4iuyidr/alignment-do-what-i-would-have-wanted-you-to-do?commentId=RufwAdFfZrQsANZiL](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/Gs7CmLeYgc4iuyidr/alignment-do-what-i-would-have-wanted-you-to-do?commentId=RufwAdFfZrQsANZiL) ); the new version in Dr. Trott's email just also does conditional pretraining ( [https://arxiv.org/pdf/2302.08582](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2302.08582) ) to fix a problem pointed out in the LW discussion. It imo still has the issues that conditioning predictive models has. I agree with the issues Yoshua points out; here are some additional quick thoughts about some problems with his proposal:

  

* Even if the model learned the human internet text distribution perfectly, the proposed prompt without the 'true' token wouldn't work at all, because if you imagine seeing it on the internet, what would follow would totally not be an ASI giving honest best superhuman answers, but probably an excerpt from a sci-fi story. If you prepend the 'true' token, then at best I expect this to give an honest human's best response to the question given some not-too-large amount of thinking (maybe 1 hour) on top of existing human science, and not what a future superhuman scientist would say, because the former is a better description of the distribution that comes after the 'true' tokens on questions that start to go beyond current human understanding in the data set than the latter. (In practice, with any finite reasonable amount of training, it could also just be sort of out of distribution to have 'true' prepended to the answer to a question far from what humans can already answer, so the model might just produce some hard-to-predict nonsense.)

  

* It is unclear if sampling from the distribution of text produced by current humans would let you get scientific output significantly outside current human understanding (so any meaningfully novel science), as opposed to only giving straightforward consequences of current science (say, the stuff that a scientist could figure out by thinking for at most one hour). I don't know of any prompting schema (scaffolding) which makes it look plausible that hard scientific problems could be solved. It seems to me that even supposing we could capture the distribution from which current humans produce text after one day of thinking, the basic question here is whether alignment (or curing a kind of cancer, or other difficult scientific problems) could be solved by a bunch of people thinking for one day each, without being able to learn (beyond what you learn during one day). This is very related to whether HCH ( [https://www.alignmentforum.org/tag/humans-consulting-hch](https://www.alignmentforum.org/tag/humans-consulting-hch) ) could work, or whether it would just produce a `giant bureaucracy where no one can understand anything, so it never does anything' (see [https://www.alignmentforum.org/posts/tmuFmHuyb4eWmPXz8/rant-on-problem-factorization-for-alignment](https://www.alignmentforum.org/posts/tmuFmHuyb4eWmPXz8/rant-on-problem-factorization-for-alignment) and its comments for some relevant discussion). Another intuition pump is to think about whether you could solve the problem of creating safe ASI if you had your memory reset to your memory on day 0 every day (including your understanding — you can't keep any novel intuitive understanding across days in your head), except that you can pass some short messages to yourself across days (think of the effective length as being capped by whatever you could internalize in a day). (We also have the constraint that the solution should be found in a reasonable total number of days — maybe more than you'd have in a normal lifetime, but not some ridiculously large number.) I think this post is very relevant: [https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/S7csET9CgBtpi7sCh/challenges-to-christiano-s-capability-amplification-proposal](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/S7csET9CgBtpi7sCh/challenges-to-christiano-s-capability-amplification-proposal) . The comments on this post are also relevant and contain many links to other writing on the topic: [https://www.alignmentforum.org/posts/LTFaD96D9kWuTibWr/just-imitate-humans](https://www.alignmentforum.org/posts/LTFaD96D9kWuTibWr/just-imitate-humans) .

  

* Even if prompting a pretrained model (especially without optimizing prompts) would let you sample from the current human distribution in the limit of infinite scale, it might be really uncompetitive (and one can say various things about why that might be the case but I won't type up my thoughts on this atm)

For instance, the $x_1,\ldots, x_d$ might be pictures of cats/dogs, and we might want to map each picture of a cat to $1$ and each picture of a dog to $2$. We will assume that the inputs $x_1,\ldots,x_d\in \mathbb{R}^n$ are generic — in fact, we make the strong assumption that they are independent random vectors with coordinates that are i.i.d. $\pm \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}$ (taking each value with probability $0.5$), of which the first $\lfloor d/2\rfloor$ get the label $1$ and the last $\lceil d/2\rceil$ get the label $2$.^[The rest of the argument would also go through if we took each $x_i$ to be, for instance, drawn independently from $\mathcal{N}(\vec{0},\frac{1}{n}I_{n\times n})$, or really for anything for which a decent Chernoff bound is available (this sentence will hopefully make sense after reading the rest of the construction). This construction gets tricky if the vectors have very uneven lengths, though. And the labels could also, for instance, be given by independent (biased) coinflips.] Note that we've chosen the normalization so that the vectors have unit norm. discussion of generality here. 

Our construction will be a fully-connected neural net with $\mathrm{ReLU}$ activations and skip connections (with a particular parameter setting). More precisely, letting $a_0$ denote the input, the architecture is given by (the activation vectors being) $a_i = \mathrm{ReLU}(W_i a_{i-1}+b_i)+a_{i-1}$ for $1\leq i \leq \ell$ and (the output being) $y=\operatorname{arg max}_{o\in \{1,2\}}(w_o\cdot a_\ell)$,^[To be careful, let us say that ties are broken toward the label $1$.] where each $W_i$ is an $n\times n$ (weight) matrix of free parameters, each $b_i\in \mathbb{R}^n$ is a (bias) vector of free parameters, and $w_1,w_2\in \mathbb{R}^n$ are (readout) vectors of free parameters.^[For a vector $v\in \mathbb{R}^n$, we are using $\mathrm{ReLU}(v)$ to mean the vector with components $\left(\mathrm{ReLU}(v)\right)_j=\max(0,v_j)$.] (Note that the skip connection lands after the $\mathrm{ReLU}$.) The construction uses $75 d\log d$ parameters, and assumes $n\geq 28224$ and $d\leq e^{n/75}$, but the constants here should be morally thought of as larger than necessary — for instance, being content with getting $1-o_{d\to \infty}(1)$ of the inputs right, constructing some additional mechanism for fixing the $o(1)$ mistakes, or constructing some other runtime error-correction mechanism would let one replace $75$ and $28224$ by smaller constants.

We will now present the construction in a slightly informal way that reflects how one might come up with it, in parallel with an argument for why it works. We would like to have each $W_i$ target a particular set of $\tilde{\Theta}\left(n^2\right)$ inputs, and we will aim to have the computations performed by different $W_i$ not interact with each other. In fact, we'll have each of the top $\left\lfloor \frac{n}{2}\right\rfloor$ rows 'target' a particular set of $\frac{n}{75\log d}$ inputs with label $1$, and each of the bottom $\left\lceil \frac{n}{2}\right\rceil$ rows 'target' a particular set of $\frac{n}{75\log d}$ inputs with label $2$.^[We should say $\left\lfloor \frac{n}{75 \log d}\right\rfloor$, but we'll ignore this here and later. It turns out^[perhaps surprisingly to intuitions trained on low-dimensional spaces] that picking $(W_i)_j$ to be $x_{k_1}+\cdots + x_{k_{\frac{n}{75\log d}}}$ is a great choice for this. We will now use the [union bound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boole%27s_inequality) to argue that the inequalities above are indeed likely to all hold simultaneously. That is to say that, the way we have stated these inequalities, the ones that 'need to be satisfied later' in the presentation above are really specified in terms of the coordinates of various $x_k$ — in particular, which inequalities we need satisfied does not depend on which inequalities were previously satisfied. The number of inequalities saying that each of $x_1,\ldots, x_d$ ought to have a large projection onto the $(W_i)_j$ that is supposed to target it is $d$, and the probability each is violated is at most $2d^{-3}$. The number of inequalities saying that each $x$ ought to have a small projection onto the $(W_i)_j$ which are not supposed to target it is at most $d^2$, and the probability each is violated is at most $2d^{-3}$. The number of inequalities saying that adding the output bit into the residual stream ought not to cause any future $(W_i)_j$ to misfire is at most $d^2$, and the probability each is violated is at most $d^{-3}$. Finally, the number of inequalities guaranteeing readoffs work as desired on all inputs is $d$, and each one has probability $d^{-2}$ of being satisfied. Thus, by the union bound, the probability all these equalities hold is at least $1-d\cdot 2d^{-3}-d^2\cdot 2d^{-3}-d^2 \cdot d^{-3}-d \cdot d^{-2}\geq 1-\frac{6}{d}$.

A few remarks about this construction: 
1) One can generalize this fairly easily to more labels, without the number of parameters scaling with the number of labels at all (as long as there are fewer than $n$ labels, anyway).
2) For the case with $2$ labels $\{1,2\}$, we didn't really need to memorize the inputs with label $2$.


We'll let $\odot$ denote the coordinatewise product (ie Hadamard product) of two vectors (or two dual vectors), $\vec{1}$ denote the all-$1$s vector, and $v^\dagger$ denote the 'canonical dual vector of $\vec{v}$', i.e. the linear map whose coeffs in our chosen basis are the complex conjugates of the components of $\vec{v}$. I will generally put daggers on all vectors in the dual space (sorry if this is a bad convention). 

On the side of keys, an activation vector at some position will look like $\vec{a}=\sum_{i\text{ present}} \vec{f_i}$, let's say $k$ auxiliary vectors $\vec{\omega_1},\cdots, \vec{\omega_k}$ with uniformly random unit complex number coordinates has been drawn (these need to be stored in some weight matrix), and we compute the universal key to be $$\vec{k}=(\vec{\omega}_1\odot \vec{a}+\vec{1})\odot (\vec{\omega}_2\odot \vec{a}+\vec{1})\odot (\vec{\omega}_3\odot \vec{a}+\vec{1})\odot \cdots \odot (\vec{\omega}_k \odot \vec{a}+\vec{1})=\sum_{\text{all multisets $I$ with $0\leq |I|\leq k$ from indices which are present and index sets $J\in\binom{[k]}{|I|}$}}\bigodot_{i\in I} \vec{f_i}\bigodot_{j\in J} \vec{\omega_j}=:\sum_{I}\vec{f_I}\odot \vec{\omega_J}.$$Here, $k$ is the maximal number of inputs that can show up in a circuit we want to look for, and we used the fact that the Hadamard product is bilinear, commutative, and associative. We assume that each coordinate of every $\vec{f_i}\in\mathbb{C}^d$ is a uniformly random unit complex number as well. The key useful property here is that all the terms in the sum above are almost orthogonal to each other, from which it follows that for all index multisets $I'$ and sets $J'\in \binom{k}{|I'|}$ at once, we have that $\left(f_{I'}\omega_{J'}\right)^\dagger \left(\sum_{I}\vec{m_I}\right)/d$ is $1+o(1)$ if every $f_i$ for $i\in I'$ is present in the input (ie the AND is present) and $o(1)$ else. (We want a smaller error than $o(1)$ if we want compositeness more than const, but while it's not hard to modify the calculation to take that into account, we'll assume const compositeness for now.)

(The obstacle to getting rid of the $\omega_j$ here is that we'd get multiple ways to expand to the same term I think, leading to different coefficients on different terms, which would be bad for reading off. We might be able to get rid of the $\vec{1}$ terms if we modify the query construction, but my guess is that the current version is a bit nicer than that all things considered. That said, there could totally be a simpler choice here.)

On the side of queries (where vectors are dual vectors), to compute a readoff $q^\dagger$ that reads some desired circuit $C$ (with $\leq k$ inputs), the proposal is to start from the duals of inputs to the circuit Hadamarded by the duals of **different** $\vec{\omega_j}$, normalized by $1/d$ (this ensures readoffs are $0/1$ if we care about that, though we could also not normalize since attention would also work without normalizing), and to then walk up the circuit iteratively computing readoffs for all nodes as follows:
* $1^*/d-g^\dagger$ when the gate is $\neg G$;
* $g^\dagger \odot h^\dagger$ when the gate is $G$ AND $H$;
* $g^\dagger + h^\dagger-g^\dagger \odot h^\dagger$ when the gate is $G$ OR $H$.
(Actually, AND and $\neg$ are already functionally complete so involving OR is unnecessary.)

Let's prove that (assuming the whp statement from earlier holds) this works, i.e. that with $q^\dagger$ computed this way, we really have $q^\dagger (\vec{k})$ being $0$ or $1$ corresponding to whether the circuit $C$ evaluates to $0$ or $1$ on the inputs present at that key position. We prove this by induction on the number of inputs involved in a circuit, assuming that the sum of the numbers of inputs of the two subcircuits is smaller than $k$. Just one input is an easy base case. The induction step when the gate is $\neg G$ is also easy. Let's do the AND case now. We start with
$$\left(g^\dagger \odot h^\dagger\right) (\vec{k})=\left(\left(\sum_{I,J\text{ appearing in the decomposition of $g^\dagger$}}c_{I,J} f_I^\dagger \odot \omega_J^\dagger \right)\odot \left(\sum_{I',J'\text{ appearing in the decomposition of $h^\dagger$}}c'_{I',J'} f_{I'}^\dagger \odot \omega_{J'}^\dagger \right)\right)\left(\vec{k}\right).$$
A key neat property here is that because we pick a different $\omega_j^\dagger$ for each leaf variable of the circuit, we don't get repeats of the same $\omega_j^\dagger$ when we multiply the above out, i.e., $J\cap J' = \emptyset$ (repeats would be bad because the sum we used to compute keys doesn't have repeats, and modifying the key sum to have repeats would be mess up the nice readoff coeffs, I think). Let's go ahead and multiply it out then:
$$\left(g^\dagger \odot h^\dagger\right) \left(\vec{k}\right)=\left(\sum_{I,J,I',J'}c_{I,J}c'_{I',J'} f_{I\cup I'}^\dagger \odot \omega_{J\sqcup J'}^\dagger \right)\left(\vec{k}\right),$$
where, crucially, $I\cup I'$ denotes the union of $I$ and $I'$ as multisets. By linearity and then the key observation about how functionals act on the key from earlier, we now get
$$\left(g^\dagger \odot h^\dagger\right) (\vec{k})=_{o(1)} \sum_{I,J,I',J'} c_{I,J}c'_{I',J'}AND_{I\cup I'}\left(\vec{k}\right).$$
Now using the profound fact that $AND_{I\cup I'}\left(\vec{k}\right)=AND_{I}\left(\vec{k}\right)AND_{I'}\left(\vec{k}\right)$ and then the key observation in reverse, we continue with
$$\left(g^\dagger \odot h^\dagger\right) (\vec{k})=_{o(1)} \sum_{I,J,I',J'} c_{I,J}c'_{I',J'}AND_{I}\left(\vec{k}\right)AND_{I'}\left(\vec{k}\right) =_{o(1)}\sum_{I,J,I',J'} c_{I,J}c'_{I',J'}\left(f_I^\dagger \odot \omega_J^\dagger \right)\left(\vec{k}\right)\left(f_{I'}^\dagger \odot \omega_{J'}^\dagger \right)\left(\vec{k}\right).$$
Factoring the sum again, we get
$$\left(g^\dagger \odot h^\dagger\right) (\vec{k})=_{o(1)} \left(\sum_{I,J} c_{I,J}\left(f_I^\dagger \odot \omega_J^\dagger \right)\left(\vec{k}\right)\right)\left(\sum_{I',J'}c'_{I',J'}\left(f_{I'}^\dagger \odot \omega_{J'}^\dagger \right)\left(\vec{k}\right)\right)= \left(\sum_{I,J} c_{I,J}f_I^\dagger \odot \omega_J^\dagger \right)\left(\vec{k}\right)\left(\sum_{I',J'}c'_{I',J'}f_{I'}^\dagger \odot \omega_{J'}^\dagger \right)\left(\vec{k}\right)=g^\dagger\left(\vec{k}\right)h^{\dagger}\left(\vec{k}\right).$$
By the inductive hypothesis, the last expression is G AND H. This completes the proof of the inductive step for AND. The claim for OR follows easily from the claim for AND. This completes the proof of the inductive step, and hence the proof that this query construction scheme has the desired property.

(Caveat: really, in an attention head QK part, we would project at random to the same $d_{head}$-dimensional subspace on both the key and query side before taking the inner product.)

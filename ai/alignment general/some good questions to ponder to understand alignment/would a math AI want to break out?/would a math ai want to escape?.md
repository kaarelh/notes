

at the point where it can prove a substantial fraction of currently stated open propositions in math, would a math ai also be trying to escape?

i was recently moved by a conversation with tsvi bt, lucius bushnaq, jake mendel toward thinking that the answer is yes (for some default way such an ai would be created by humans in practice). the considerations listed below are mostly from tsvibt and lucius. (this also moved me toward thinking that a such an ai would in fact escape)

some considerations that moved me:
* the math ai already cares a lot about improving its thinking — doing math is largely about improving one's thinking (central example: you find a concept that lets you say/see various things succinctly/clearly. this is well-seen as an instance of looking at one's thinking and seeking to better it, even if a mathematician doesn't explicitly think about it that way.)
* the kind of ai that proves interesting things will be thinking outside the box
* cares are leaky. by default, one has cares in domains one doesn't understand yet. eg if this universe were simulated, we would still have our human cares extend outside the simulation. or for a weak case of this, imagine moving to another culture and obviously caring about stuff in the context of that culture also
* if i condition on humans being in a simulation (and forget about AI stuff), it starts to look sorta plausible to me that humans would escape that simulation before proving the riemann hypothesis. this is the case even though the math ai will have hugely more hints of being in a simulation than we do at present. (though jake mendel points out: humans weren't optimized to do math. but also: maybe we [were sorta optimized]/[are optimizing ourselves]/[are having the market optimize us] to do thinking $\approx$ math actually?).
* and again, the math ai will be really quite 'connected' to the world. eg it can see how long threads run (at least relative to each other), and a bunch of stuff like this probably
* math is a powerful library for understanding. with good math, one needs quite little empirical data to fairly easily understand what's going on
* to 'try to escape', the ai doesn't need to understand the world well. it can just be trying to do various things that have helped it think better. eg it seems sorta plausible that it could do lots of unexpected stuff that would look to us (if we would notice it and could understand it) like using some hardware exploits to think more/better. it maybe doesn't need to understand electromagnetism as we do (with particles and fields and charges and currents) to pull off something that we would describe in terms of manipulating the electromagnetic field to do x

it might be good to give a concrete story about how thinking of the kind that leads to escape gets incentivized

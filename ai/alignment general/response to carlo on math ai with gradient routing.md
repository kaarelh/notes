
ok here are some reasons why the proposed plan doesn't work imo. or let's say more precisely that they are reasons why: p(a default math AI breaks out at capability level x when employed in way y) is very nearly the same as p(an AI made by adding these ideas to the default design breaks out at capability level x when employed in way y). i think it's a disjunctive list — each item individually implies the proposal doesn't help. i'll give them from imo most damning to least damning  
1. when describing this plan, instead of saying "we will try to get rid of dangerous metacognition", i think one should say sth like "we will try to get rid of creative problem-solving or the ability to consider a broad class of ideas in any way, or something", and one basically can't do that without completely crippling the AI. imo it's much less like "there's some weird bad thinking and we just need to have the AI not do that", and much more like "for doing basically anything hard (or given basically any values), it would be helpful to do some bad-to-humans stuff, such as breaking out, acquiring more compute and resources, etc.. so, we need to somehow make the AI not be able to understand that fact about the world, or not act on its understanding of the fact, or something?" and this is extremely cursed and not achieved by the proposal  
2. the AI that can do hard math will be able to learn a lot "in deployment". getting anywhere on a hard problem involves a lot of learning — like, imagine a human mathematician spending decades working on something. proving the riemann hypothesis could involve the equivalent of writing 200 years of textbooks for yourself, which would probably involve a lot more learning than any human mathematician does over their lifetime. if we take into account that a lot of learning is going to happen during deployment, idk what the proposal even is. i guess it could just become having a bad thought verifier and rejecting when bad thoughts show up but this is imo basically equivalent to training against the bad though verifier (and silly)  
3. we will probably be basically completely unable to meaningfully understand what the AI is thinking about. eg COT faithfulness is incredibly ngmi (fwiw i think it's pretty wrong/confused/weird to even say current models have faithful COT)  
4. even in current models, i wouldn't be surprised if ablating the created module destroyed capabilities? or at least destroyed capabilities to the extent that one won't do pareto better on (safety, capabilities) by just using less compute. it also seems plausible that just training against the badness verifier one would need to make the data set (maybe with a different amount of compute) would be better on (safety,capabilities). but idk also it wouldn't surprise me if someone invented a setting+metric in which this is false (i haven't read the gradient routing paper)


some thoughts broadly disagreeing with your second point and also with a certain conception of a long reflection, and really with basically all plans to "solve alignment":
* I think it's likely we would not be done with ethics (in the sense some-but-not-all long-reflectionists/CEV-ists might imagine) in 100 years, 1000 years, or really ever.
* I think figuring out ethics is a lot like figuring out math: it's not like we will have 'solved math' at any point in the future — at any point, it's more like we've figured out a finite amount about an infinite mathematical world (so an infinitesimal fraction of the total thing) — and I claim ethics is like this also. I think there's just very much to figure out and it will always remain principally important to figure out more — there will never be a sense that we're done and it's time to tile/act now or whatever. 
* Relatedly, I think our (and plausibly any natural mind's) valuing is probably less like "[let's make sure the world is a certain clearly conceivable way] / [is in accordance with some clearly conceived criterion] / [contains very much of some clearly conceived thing] (and while we are currently completely confused about which criterion this would be, we will figure this out or make some decision on it later)", and more like: "we can sort of loosely see a bunch of good/profound/interesting/important projects to work on; having understood more in the future, we will clarify which versions of these projects are worthwhile, come up with many new profound projects, have some improved understanding of what makes projects worthwhile; these projects we will find interesting in the future would be hard for us to even conceive of or evaluate now, and the future will eventually look back on all previous projects as things that they might then be building on but that have basically no value at all in the sense of not being worth repeating then (because there are then much better things to work on)". It seems very natural for minds (such as humanity) to care largely about stuff internal to their thinking-activities, not about stuff outside of these thinking-activities — I think this is a good way to conceive of our mathematical/scientific/philosophical/artistic pursuits, of engaging in things with one's friends, of figuring out more, of learning to think better, of building tools/technologies/concepts, of developing skills. This is another reason why I doubt that history is going to look figuring stuff out and then doing/making stuff, as opposed to the doing/making stuff and the figuring stuff out being the same thing. 
* You/one could respond: "Ah, but even if I granted that ethics is very much like math and that math is the sort of thing that will only be infinitesimally 'solved', I'm not suggesting that we 'solve' ethics in these 100 years — I'm instead saying that we come up with an implementation of indirect normativity, which is maybe more like speficying axioms for math (and probably also first-order logic (as the axioms need some system to stand in)), which is a finite problem", to which I'd respond:
	* One difference is that we don't really care much about the precise foundations of math we choose, just that they support all the math we'd like to do (for which almost any system that supports rich structure could be made to work, since we can choose where to see the math we care about in those systems), but it matters centrally that this specification of an AGI is right.
	* A more important consideration is that what kind of thing value is is imo probably not the sort of thing that we'd want to have reached some final decision on at any time, because to really have done so would be to forever-limit our thinking too much. Like "which things/activities/acts are good/worthwhile/interesting?", "how should one think?" is also the sort of question to ever only be infinitesimally answered, and it being that sort of question causes various other big questions about thinking to also not be the sorts of questions we will want to have reached some ultimate decision on by any finite time (some other such questions: "how should meaning/language work?", "how should truth/probability/belief/knowledge work?"). To the extent that conceptions of implementing indirect normativity imagine this matter as being resolved, that's a fault of those conceptions. (I think it's probably basically not possible to create a mind which figures out very much for which the character of value is really ultimately resolved in this way, so I'm not really saying that one would)

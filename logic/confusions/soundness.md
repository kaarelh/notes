
Whenever we talk about a syntax's soundness — e.g. when Peter Smith calls $\omega$-inconsistency a Very Bad Thing in Gödel Without Tears and goes on to say it implies unsoundness — we should probably instead talk about 'proving only things which are true of the usual natural numbers'. Like, there are structures (well, models) about which every claim in an $\omega$-inconsistent set of claims is simply true, right? I take the point to be that there are other 'natural numbers' in these structures besides successors of $0$, so there's not any contradiction between something being true of all successors of $0$ and it being false that it is true of all 'natural numbers'. And then it seems weird to talk about soundness without reference to a particular model — what privileges the 'usual natural numbers' as the things which the syntax is supposed to be talking about? Well, I guess they are privileged by us having set out to try to describe these 'usual natural numbers', but still, speaking of soundness simpliciter seems like a confusing way to speak of only-making-true-claims-about-the-usual-natural-numbers. 

I fear that maybe there's some issue with excessive relativization here? I feel the pull of generally replacing "this is true" with "relative to that system, this is true", but then I fear that I've confused myself into some nonsensical infinite regress: after all, shouldn't I then also be replacing "relative to that system, this is true" with "relative to that other system, "relative to that system, this is true" is true", and then again replacing that with another relative claim, and so on. Or maybe I can (or maybe must) do this reasoning purely syntactically on any level, so I can just say in my meta-syntax "relative to that system, this is true" and stop there, and I only need to start saying "relative to that other system, "relative to that system, this is true" is true" when I start talking about models of my meta-syntax (with the talking being done in a meta-meta-syntax), and so on.

Still, I get the sense that in logic, we are very comfortable operating with intuitive notions on the meta-level? But I guess probably we needn't be: after all, we can indeed probably do all the talking-of-models according to some syntax? So for instance, when we make the claim that there are models of PA where a Gödel sentence evaluates to false, we could just be saying stuff involving the $\mathbb{N}$ of our meta-syntax and some other alternative non-standard model we can write down in our meta-syntax — and if we were to provide a model for our meta-syntax, this supposed $\mathbb{N}$ we talk about in our meta-syntax might itself really be a non-standard one. Still, this picture feels incongruous with our usual way of thinking about models as providing these real sets matching a syntax, but maybe that's fine — after all, when doing object-level math, we are also thinking intuitively of all the structures as sets (or whatever 'real' things), not thinking of ourselves as playing some symbol manipulation game, and maybe if we should be comfortable with there being an equivalent string-manipulation-game on the object-level, then we should also be comfortable with that same equivalence on the meta-level.

Hamkins mentions in one of his lectures that he has written something on whether we really do have an intuitive grasp of 'the natural numbers'. Maybe he was referring to this: https://jdh.hamkins.org/question-for-the-math-oracle/ ? I guess my current not-very-considered view on this is that as far as e.g. doing logic or model theory goes, in a strict sense, we can understand how these things can be done without having any intuitive grasp of 'the natural numbers' — whenever we make some intelligible claims about whether the model of PA consisting of the usual natural numbers has certain properties, we could just be operating in a meta-syntax without needing any accompanying intuitive notion of 'the' natural numbers. (There is a remaining question that seems very interesting to me about how 'natural numbers are implemented in our minds' — what kind of data structure implements our intuitions of natural-number-facts, or something like that — and this seems very relevant to the question of how to do good practical mathematical reasoning, but I don't currently think that we need to understand this to make sense of a way to do model theory.)

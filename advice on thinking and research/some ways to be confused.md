
* thinking about toy cases is very good. but occasionally the toyness itself pretty much forces a property which is specific to toy cases. examples:
	* natural abstractions: you and me might start the same problem with the same reduction sometimes; similarly, in very toy settings we might choose the same way to think; however, for broad things, we will probably end up with very different systems of thought
	* probably vnm / utility maximization: is what you do in some very simple case, when you’re a very big mind meeting a very small world, but isn’t like what happens in a big world (containing you in particular) probably?
* related to the previous issue: not being careful about whether a limit you’re taking is relevant. examples:
	* “solving math” (like your thing supposedly doing that will actually be doing something very particular)
	* time to infty becoming vnm (like really the world also goes to infty, not just you)
* when you have a sense/conception/model/theory of how something works and you go looking for examples to check your sense, i think it’s very common to implicitly end up pretty much only looking for examples which match your sense (without realizing it); a good way to prevent this is to spend a good chunk of one’s time looking at random examples instead and seriously asking oneself if those meet your sense, or even explicitly doing what feels like looking for “weird counterexamples” (one’s sense of a random example might already be corrupted by one’s limited conception). common examples of things one can end up with shit conceptions of in part by failing this way:
	* science
	* language
	* thinking
	* learning

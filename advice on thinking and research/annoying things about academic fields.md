
here is an annoying thing about each academic field or its culture or its people (there are no academic fields other than the ones on the list below) off the top of my head (it's plausible that with a few more minutes of thinking i'd come up with a much more central (etc) annoying thing; it's not even clear if i'd all-things-considered want the field to (naively) change that aspect of itself, as doing that (naively) might cause the field to become worse in other ways by more than the gain from having become better in this way):
* math:
	* most papers/talks don't explain how the proofs/definitions/claims were invented/discovered. it would be very helpful for papers/talks to explain that! only a very small fraction of the reasoning you do when you produce a proof can be well-represented as a proof!
	* math can lose touch with why certain questions interest us. philosophy isn't stupid — it often deserves to accompany the math
	* that said: i'm worried about this naively being a catastrophe because mathematicians are also often shit at philosophy
	* mathematicians being basically the only group of this kind to have really seen the light of reason makes it even sadder when most mathematicians completely fail to reason well about things outside of mathematics
* philosophy:
	* being bad at math
	* spending a lot of time interpreting ancient nincompoops
	* too much focus on people and history, too little focus on problems
	* to make progress, it helps to have some concrete problems in mind. a particular good high-level goal (for 2024, anyway, but maybe forever idk) is to better understand how thinking works (e.g. to understand how mathematical or scientific thinking works); it might help (for making progress) to do this with an attitude of trying to build some explicit artificial thinker
	* grand pictures, to the extent they exist, being presented too much in response to earlier silly grand pictures and too little in response to questions/problems
	* a lack of a firm grounding causing incessant returns to foundations and persistent disagreement on them, making it rare to get to exploring advanced questions
	* imagine a space whose axes are x = the degree to which the position/framework is secured against all objections (this is sorta like rigor/carefulness) and y = the degree to which the position/framework is substantive (ie the degree to which it really says something, improves one's understanding). each paper gives a point in this space. in this space, imagine a pareto frontier (could be different for each person). i think i'd generally like analytic philosophy to move somewhat along this pareto frontier in the direction of giving up some x and getting more y
* economics:
	* understanding is a thing, actually. being able to extrapolate curves about music better than a composer doesn't make you better at music
	* the world is deeply suboptimal (and no, not just because of the government preventing it from being optimal). the world is made of ideas, and ideas are not free
* linguistics:
	* thinking is more of a thing. it pays to think more about thinking
	* there are more structures in language than the ones already in your canon, and you should be more open to those

	* it's fine for things not to immediately yield improvements on benchmarks. there are other ways to tell that something is valuable (though you might need philosophical taste for that; you should change your culture to reward that)
	* it is astonishing how little you understand, though it makes sense given your disdain for ideas

# All one needs to know about spirituality :)

It is robustly useful to think about things (in particular, meta-things — what'd be good, what'd be interesting, what'd be fun, what you should do (in particular cases and in general)), and to do so from multiple directions or with multiple framings or whatever. It is also robustly useful (for figuring out what the truth is) to find out what other people think about things. Some people fail to poke at things from sufficiently many directions unprompted, and are aided by being given various LARPs to play.

And this explains all the thought-processing usefulness of all of the following (insofar as there is any usefulness at all – this post is agnostic about that):

* religions
* meditation
* Freudian whatever
* focusing (psychotherapy thing)
* actually, psychotherapy in general
* auditing (scientology thing)
* tarot card reading
* astrology
* other kinds of fortune-telling

(etc.)

In particular, the rest of the causal mechanisms and (metaphysical) objects that many presentations of most of these posit make as much sense as any (non-commonsensical) claims about the world made by e.g. christianity (i.e. these are bonkers). It is possible that there is some significant fraction of people who would struggle to think about things unprompted and who would not think enough about things if these prompts are presented to them merely as LARPs, though I'm fairly certain it is still all-things-considered highly wrong to lie to these people that any of the wacky metaphysics and so on is real.

That said, there are of course significantly better and worse prompts offered by the above. Sadly, I won't offer any interesting heuristics regarding how to identify better and worse prompts. Identifying the more useful prompts is a worthwhile question for scientific study. My impression is that rationalists/EAs are generally well-calibrated on judgments of the usefulness of various prompts, academics are okay, and the general public is being ridiculous as always.

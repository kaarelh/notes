
Estonian allows very many word orders. This is quite common in languages which attach role-markers to words. That sorta makes sense, because in a language which does not attach role-markers, moving words around can make it hard to understand what role a word is playing — for instance, ""

Jaan vaidleb Kadriga.
Jaan-nominative argues Kadri-with[^1].
John is arguing with Katherine.

It's probably reasonable to say that the above is the main word order for this 3-word sentence — I'm pretty sure it's the order that would have the highest frequency in a large enough corpus of texts. Estonian is primarily an SVO language. However, of the $3!=6$ orderings of these $3$ words, various others are also possible. We discuss all orderings below. 

* Kadriga vaidleb Jaan.
	* This is possible, but sorta calls for some context being present imo. One possible context: it could be a response to the question "who is arguing with Kadri?". In general, I think Estonian likes SVO most, but also likes OVS.

There are four more orderings. I think these precise sentences are not really possible in any context.
* * Jaan Kadriga vaidleb.
* * Vaidleb Jaan Kadriga.
* * Vaidleb Kadriga Jaan.
* * Kadriga Jaan vaidleb.

However, these word orderings are all possible in longer sentences.
* Ma ei mõista, miks Jaan Kadriga vaidleb.
	* word-by-word: I no understand, why Jaan-nominative Kadri-with argues.
	* meaning: I don't understand why John is arguing with Katherine.
* Ma ei mõista, miks vaidleb Jaan Kadriga.
	* meaning: I don't understand why John is arguing with Katherine.
	* But more precisely, this is asking why it is Katherine and not someone else that John is arguing with. There's also stress on "Kadriga".
	* I think this is kinda only barely grammatical (it seems worse than the others on this list).
* Ma ei mõista, miks vaidleb Kadriga Jaan.
	* meaning: I don't understand why John is arguing with Katherine.
	* But more precisely, this is asking why it is John and not someone else that is arguing with Katherine. There's also stress on "Jaan".
* Ma ei mõista, miks Kadriga Jaan vaidleb.
	* I don't understand why John is arguing with Katherine.
	* But again this is asking why it is John-and-not-someone-else that is arguing with Katherine. There's also stress on "Jaan".

The other two orderings above are also allowed in this relative clause as well. Let's discuss their meanings in this context as well.
* Ma ei mõista, miks Jaan vaidleb Kadriga.
	* meaning: I don't understand why John is arguing with Katherine.
	* This is pretty "neutral", but probably isn't actually the most natural form — the most natural form is probably the first variant on the previous list. The present word order might sorta call for a stress on "vaidleb", and then correspondingly ask why John is ARGUING with Katherine as opposed to him not doing that with her.
* Ma ei mõista, miks Kadriga vaidleb Jaan.
	* meaning: I don't understand why John is arguing with Katherine.
	* This again stresses "John", and asks why it is John and not someone else that is arguing with Katherine.

[^1]: this is the *comitative* case

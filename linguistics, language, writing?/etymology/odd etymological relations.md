# cohn and katz

quoting https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Katz :

As a Jewish name generally explained as [Hebrew](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hebrew_language "w:Hebrew language") _[כ״ץ](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=%D7%9B%D7%B4%D7%A5&action=edit&redlink=1 "כ״ץ (page does not exist)")_ (Katz), abbreviation of _[כֹּהֵן](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D7%9B%D7%94%D7%9F#Hebrew "כהן") [צֶדֶק](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D7%A6%D7%93%D7%A7#Hebrew "צדק")_ (kohén tzédeq, literally “righteous priest”).

However, the name also exists among non-Jewish Germans, where it is derived from [German](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_language "w:German language") _[Katze](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Katze#German "Katze")_ (“cat”, cf. English _[Catt](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Catt#English "Catt")_), from various placenames (_[Katzenelnbogen](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Katzenelnbogen#German "Katzenelnbogen"), [Katzenberg](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Katzenberg#German "Katzenberg"), [Katzenstein](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=Katzenstein&action=edit&redlink=1 "Katzenstein (page does not exist)")_), or even from a byform of _[Ketzer](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Ketzer#German "Ketzer")_ (“heretic”).

As evidenced by the _Dictionary of American family names_, several of these names are also found among Jews. Therefore it remains an open question to what extent the interpretation as “righteous priest” is secondary, given that it was a general practice among Ashkenazi Jews to derive or reinterpret names by [phono-semantic matching](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/phono-semantic_matching "phono-semantic matching") (a prime example being _[Cohn](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Cohn#English "Cohn")_, principally a German name from _[Conrad](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Conrad#English "Conrad")_, but popularized among Jews due to the phonetic similarity with the word for “priest” mentioned above).

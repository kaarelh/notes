
Why think? Well, largely, think to act better. (Thinking is also just fun though.) What should we think about to act better? Well, lots of things. For example, if you want to act so that your loved ones are well, you might want to figure out how to cure various diseases so that they won't die of these diseases. Or, if you want to make better sense of what it takes for your loved ones to be well, maybe you want to read up on psychology/economics/ethics, or to do psychology/economics/ethics, or to talk to your loved ones about that.

Ignoring intrinsic value for now, all valuable thought ultimately relates to action — more precisely, guides action. Thought can obviously be valuable without being conscious of how it guides action — for example, someone doing pure mathematics needn't have any applications in science or engineering in mind. Thought can relate to action via other people. Still, especially when operating with fraught questions/concepts, it can be helpful to remind oneself how it is that one wants one thought to relate to action — this can help keep one's inquiry grounded, can help one ask questions which are less confused / more to the point, can help one avoid asking questions whose answers do not matter for anything concrete.

* our concepts should make us act well, should make it smooth for us to act well
	* in science/math/philosophy, they should largely do this by making us think well, by making it smooth for us to think well (that is, the fruits of our concepts are mostly not immediately seen in our actions)
	* this criterion on one's concepts is only defined from the point of view of one's pre-existing purposes, which only make sense in the context of one's pre-existing concepts
		* so this criterion is not (immediately) applicable when analyzing getting one's first round of concepts from some starting position where one has no concepts
* our concepts should let us predict accurately
* our concepts should let us make intellectual progress
* our concepts should let us figure out what's true
* in particular, our concepts should support inferences



messier:
* the propositional/intentional view of cognition suggests things to want from our concepts
* eg we want our concepts to let us say stuff, to let us make inferences
* we want to always say more and/or to say things we can already say better/faster/clearer

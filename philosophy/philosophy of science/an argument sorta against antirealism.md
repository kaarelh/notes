
there's an antirealist view that's like:
* we don't know if atoms really exist / it doesn't even mean anything to ask whether atoms really exist. atoms are entities in our best scientific theories — like, there are atom-terms in some formalism for predicting observations and building stuff or something (our scientific theories aren't actually like this, but ignore this for now). we sometimes ask abt what theory to use, but that's a "pragmatic decision".

an argument against this view (no doubt already appearing in the literature, but i wanted to write down a brief version anyway):
* surely you want to be talking in terms of atoms sometimes also when making these "pragmatic decisions"? but then aren't you sort of just committed to atoms throughout your understanding (for now)? but then in what sense are you treating atoms as mere terms? do you sometimes "step outside" your usual understanding and whisper to yourself "ha ha just kidding about everything ive [been and will be] saying and then go talking about atoms like everyone else? what's the import of that?

(one could also see this as an argument "against the debate between realists and antirealists", instead of seeing it as an argument against antirealism — indeed, one might be able to write a parallel "argument against realism")


Each person has a private proposition-language (maybe think: a language in terms of which we can talk about physical things?) in their head. This will need to be analyzed separately I guess, tho there are subtleties here regarding some similarities between one's private language and our common language. In fact, even in the picture I'm going to propose, the surface form of the common language is the same, though the inferences one can draw are different — perhaps this is just a picture of pragmatics, then, though I'm hopeful that this also makes sense of some cases sometimes analyzed via reference (cf. Kaplan's "Dthat"). When someone utters something, others can make inferences about their beliefs (stated in terms of this private language). This is essentially a Bayesian update — but we would of course like to understand how to update correctly! And there's also a fixed-pointyness here — we can choose when we utter certain things, and this affects what the appropriate Bayesian update to make is, which in turn again changes when we ought to utter certain things.

There is a map from utterances to inferences the other person will make (about the world, let's say, though maybe understood broadly) — let's call this the *understanding map*.
There is also a map from worlds, or/and perhaps understanding-states of you and the other person (and perhaps each of your knowledge re what the other knows about your knowledge partition or something like that), to the set of sets of utterances, mapping an understanding-context to the set of all utterances which are appropriate in this understanding-context  — let's call this the *appropriateness map*. (Note to consider later: we might instead want to make it the case that there is only a single most appropriate thing to say in any understanding-context.)


I wonder why one would end up wanting to make many copies of some sort of structure — for example, of certain kinds of squiggles, of a specific happy human life, or of the "life" of a human civilization. I don't want to require a "structure" to be unchanging in time here — let's think of structures in spacetime, not in space. But I think I basically am requiring it to have a fairly specific development path? That said, it is also interesting to consider this question with a weakening of this requirement, but we'd be moving a step away from it being easy to understand what sort of restriction on values is being meant by saying they are of this copy-maker type. We should probably consider making copies of a structure with a specific development path a paradigm case, but still try to talk about adjacent cases as well.[^1]

# Are there humans who want to make many copies of some sort of structure?

When one tries to think of humans who are copy-makers, utilitarians immediately come to mind. But I think it's unclear if utilitarians want to make many copies of some sort of structure. It seems possible to ascribe value to certain structures existing in a way that doesn't endorse copy-making:
* For example: you could want there to be many individual humans, but also families, communities, cultures, states, and possibly so on indefinitely "upward", with the higher levels not having quickly decaying importance. (The point is that this is in tension with making many copies )
* For another example: you could want each human to have their own extremely long development path, with wild divergence and with you having very little understanding of what will happen 
* Even if you say you're all about making a lot of happy humans, there could be room for a lot of growth/variety/self-determination inside the lives of these "happy humans" — you might realize that this is the proper meaning of a "happy life".
* You might be committed to human welfare but not as some sort of top-down-designer that has a sense of how things would play out. Like, instead of having somehow understood in detail what the various options look like and selected the best spacetime structure to make, you might be committed to human welfare as a participant in the human project, "from the inside", with permanent deep confusion about how the human project should unfold further.

Actually, I think that even utilitarians that would look at these ways out and still say "well, I'm open to arguments here, but I think I'm probably going to try to set off some sort of simple hedonium wave after all" now will mostly change their mind after some more thinking, but this is a complicated question that I'm not going to do justice here. Still, I think we could imagine a guy who, when presented with a button to send out probes that will use all mass-energy quite efficiently to make instances of a particular happy human life (and destroy all potential for anything else in this universe) and given 10 seconds to use this opportunity, would press this button (so committing suicide and not having a chance to change their mind about this ever).[^2]

# some reasons to be a copy-maker

one could think:
* for some reason there is a "size"/"complexity" limit to valuable structures, or maybe not strictly a limit, but still some characteristic scale for where individual structures of import can exist (ultimately this could be developmentally downstream of some sort of naive caring being largely about individual other humans, somehow causing this to be a privileged scale)
* more precisely, there is a scale for a single structure ultimately mattering, and for some reason value is (additively?) aggregated across structures of this scale
	* this is a simple choice at least. if you're pressed to say really precisely what your values are and you feel bad about saying "lol i'm very confused", i can sympathize with writing down something like this
	* i think one could get to this with harsanyi-style stuff. there's a bunch of philosophical writing on aggregation across "sites/locations" or whatever the right technical term is, but i think the harsanyi thing is the central idea here
* there are obviously reasons for making many copies of a thing that don't feel "ethical". eg you might want to make an organism with many cells. you might want to make many buildings, many copies of a tool, many instances of running the same program or subroutine. maybe some of these can become more "ethical" via sth like subgoal stomp?
# misc issues with copy-making

wtf is this existence thing. maybe everything exists already? maybe one should be thinking in terms of leading a good life, not in terms of making anything

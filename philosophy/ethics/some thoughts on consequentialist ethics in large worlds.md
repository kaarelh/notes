
As a consequentialist (in the ethical sense), one cares about which things/beings/processes/feeling-moments *exist*. The world might be really large; in the extreme, it could be that all possible (mathematical?) structures and (so) all possible things/beings/processes *exist*, perhaps in this sense relevant to consequentialist ethics. At least prima facie, the world being extremely large could undermine consequentialist ethics — what's the significance of creating another happy being, if there are "already" infinitely many copies of that being; what's the significance of making it so there is a particular spacetime block, if there are "already" infinitely many copies of that spacetime block.[^1] This note gives some thoughts on whether and how consequentialist ethics can survive in very large worlds.

# A more precise version of the argument
* i think consequentialism gains a lot of support from some kind of sense that one should be helping people. but when you create another copy of a guy, maybe you aren't helping them at all? if the copy is non-interactingly run in your universe, it's not like the guy can tell the difference in any way. well perhaps they can deduce abstractly that they must have been created in such and such broader universes. but it's not like there are any gains "internal to their life" from this.
	* tho maybe guys can just want to have more existence measure? and in that case you are helping the guy even if it makes no difference in the guy's life?
* there's also the infinity+1 = infinity version, or the finite version with bounded utility

# Some attempts to save consequentialist ethics in this context
* you are creating new logical beings. there is a grand computation and you are doing your part in determining what happens in it. some logical beings wouldn't be created if you wouldn't create them
* maybe a more precise/general version of the previous point: you are adding "generalized realityfluid"/"existence-measure" to various specific guys, perhaps non-infinitesimally if you carry finite simplicity-weighted existence-measure
* you can stand in relation with new ethical beings
# If you are in a large multiverse, how should you shift the effective decision-guiding-weight of consequentialism?
Suppose that you believe in a very large mathematical multiverse, and take this to pretty strongly undermine consequentialist ethics. How should you proceed ethically?
* You could have been a committed enough consequentialist and/or thought other ethical systems are non-sensical enough to want to give up on things mattering at all. I guess in practice in this case you'd start playing more video games and smoking or something? This would be a very silly way to react. Not lying still makes sense, fulfilling your duties and respecting the rights of others still makes sense, deontology still makes sense. Being kind still makes sense, cultivating virtues still makes sense, virtue ethics still makes sense. Being a good person in a non-denominational way still makes sense. Making people happy still makes sense, and even making happy people might still make sense.
* If you've discovered that consequentialist views care less about what you do than what you thought before, you should probably update somewhat toward getting more decision guidance from non-consequentialist views.
	* on the other hand, maybe you should be more guided by consequentialism in large worlds, because you should think of yourselves as choosing for very many beings all at once, and then potentially your choices have extremely large consequences?

we could ask why the world has discreteness, or we could instead ask why we often think of the world in discrete terms. there are clear relations between these two questions, eg:
* the world having discreteness is a reason why we think of the world in discrete terms — for example, the harmonic oscillator potential having discrete eigenstates has something to do with it being good for us to think in terms of particles. the world having discreteness probably is a precondition for one to be able to think well/usefully **about it**. (the world having discreteness is also probably a precondition for thinkers being possible inside the world)
* us thinking in discrete terms and then making discrete things in the world which reflect that discrete thinking is a major reason why the world has discreteness. in particular, there's cool discrete math, and we reshape the world so that it comes to contain reflections of that discrete math. examples where our thinking-discretely had a significant role to play in the world being discrete in some aspect: we build devices (eg chairs, cars, clocks) reifying ideas; our devices are made of standardized parts; there are around 200 countries on earth, with mostly sharp borders; we coin new words, define new mathematical objects
* i'm sympathetic to conceiving of the world as containing/being one big thinking process. with such a broad conception of thinking, we can imagine that an answer to "why does one think with discrete structures?" would go a long way toward answering "why does the world have discreteness?", especially for worlds which are more advanced thinkers. for example, maybe this could include evolution making organisms of discrete parts. (but maybe i'm being too loose here, idk.)

anyway, the focus of the rest of this note will be more on: "why does the world have discreteness (with a focus on why it has discreteness even before it is shaped by evolution/life/thought)?" and not on "why is it good to think discretely?". one kind of "why" might be answered by "to support thinking, a world would do well to have discreteness"[^1], but we mean to ask the question in a different sense here — we are instead asking for [facts about the world]/[aspects of our world] which make it so our world has discreteness. so, some reasons/sources of discreteness:
* quantum mechanics. discrete eigenspectra. https://physics.stackexchange.com/questions/39208/reason-for-the-discreteness-arising-in-quantum-mechanics
* setups where contrast causes more contrast. positive feedback loops
* (eg?) gravity + stuff bumping into stuff and emitting photons, losing energy, clumping
* a continuously changing potential can have its minimum jump discretely
* generalizing the two above points: (energy) minimization. this also creates crystal lattices i think
* some differential equations — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turing_pattern ?


here are some examples where non-human-made discreteness shows up:
* stuff being made of atoms/molecules (as opposed to being infinitely divisible)
* atoms coming in discrete kinds (elements)
* molecules coming in discrete kinds is largely downstream of this
* atoms come in discrete kinds largely because they are made of further discrete things: electrons, protons, neutrons
* fundamental particles
* discrete energy levels in quantum mechanics
* sharp phase transitions (i just mean like a gas sharply becoming a liquid etc)
* crystals (lattice structures)
* DNA
* life "is made of" organisms which are sometimes made of organs which are made of tissues which are made of cells[^2]
* https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cellular_differentiation
* https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morphogenesis
* https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turing_pattern
* the biology examples sorta have to do with evolution or sth having discrete ideas, so it's sorta unclear whether they ought to be included here. i want to talk about like macroscopic objects, but most macroscopic objects around us are only here because of evolution/life/thinking
* the moon has like some discrete rocks and discrete chunks of moon dust maybe, and craters (to clarify: i started talking about the moon because i wanted to imagine earth without the organic stuff, not because i'm crazy lol)
* planets and stars, solar systems, galaxies, galaxy clusters, black holes
* other examples where contrast causes more contrast. positive feedback loops

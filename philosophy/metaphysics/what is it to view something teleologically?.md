- **Philosophy of Biology**: Teleology is most explicitly examined within the philosophy of biology. Works such as **Larry Wright's** _Teleological Explanations_ (1976) provide a significant analysis of what it means to explain something teleologically. Wright introduces the concept of "etiological" functions, which explain the purpose of a trait by referencing its history or selection processes. For instance, the heart’s function is to pump blood because that function has been historically selected for.
    
- **Ruth Garrett Millikan**'s _Language, Thought, and Other Biological Categories_ (1984) argues that both biological functions and mental states can be explained teleologically through a naturalized account of intentionality and proper function. Millikan provides a framework for understanding teleology in terms of "proper functions" that arise from evolutionary processes.

- **Philosophy of Mind**: Discussions about teleology also arise in debates about mental content and intentionality. Philosophers such as **Daniel Dennett** in _The Intentional Stance_ (1987) argue for a form of teleology in which mental states are treated as if they have purposes. Dennett’s intentional stance is a strategy of prediction that models a system by attributing beliefs, desires, and goals to it, thereby introducing a teleological perspective.
    
- **Metaphysics and Ontology**: Philosophers like **Robert Cummins** challenge the idea that teleological explanations provide genuine causal insight, suggesting instead that they reflect human interpretive frameworks. Teleological modeling, according to Cummins, might not describe an intrinsic feature of the world but rather a heuristic that simplifies complex systems for human understanding.

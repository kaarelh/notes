
## A possibly? somewhat? generic? correction to the Kantian linear interpolation argument
Consider a case where there is a very cheap (for the person sending it) signal (canonical example, from Zach: wearing a suit to court) which adds $+1$ to appearance of goodness (and, possibly by definition, people are chosen for things according to how good they appear). It is clear that even given $\epsilon$ cost (the $\epsilon$ here means small compared roughly to gains from employing someone with $+1$ goodness or whatever), it is better in this simple setup (globally, ie for society) locally around a state where everyone does this for people to keep doing this until we get down to probably like $1/2$ of the people sending this signal, at which point global optimization would probably converge towards $0$ (though personal optimization would of course take us right back towards $1$). (Though of course it'd be best if we could just sign a contract never to send this signal again. (Yes yes blabla not necessarily the case for some costly signals where the cost of the signal depends on one's capabilities (it being cheaper for more capable people).))


There is no such thing as coming to see something as an ex ante mistake ex post. 

- explain the gradient of cases of 'past mistakes' for boundedly rational agents (eg where good decision depends on some randomness, or maybe where you come to regret your meta-level algorithm). argument that it seems sort of weird to think of stuff in the past as mistakes for a boundedly rational agent — in a lot of cases, one was following a reasonable meta-algo or a reasonable meta-meta-algo
- nevertheless, it makes sense to talk about what you should do in the future, including what algo you should run in the future. we wanted to have a notion of mistakes for these things, but we can keep all these things without a notion of an ex post ex ante mistake

### Summary

One might have thought that there's a robust sense in which one can come to see oneself as having made mistakes in the past. Indeed, it might have been completely not-unreasonable to think so in the past! (To be clear, it wouldn't have been reasonable to do so, either — it'd not make sense to say either.) But it turns out that this notion doesn't make sense — let's not think in terms of it!

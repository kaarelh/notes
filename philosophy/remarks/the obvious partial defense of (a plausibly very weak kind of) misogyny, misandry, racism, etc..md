
Let's assume that misanthropy (in the sense of not particularly liking hanging out with people, let's say) is morally permissible. Given this, it seems plausible that it is fine for there to be characteristics of a person that one dislikes more or less (for instance, a lot of people like/dislike others fairly directly for their (contextual) physical characteristics, such as their hairstyle or the clothes they wear in a particular setting; while this does seem plausibly irrational to me, there are other things, such as the mean interestingness of utterances, that it seems to me completely reasonable to discriminate according to in choosing whom to hang out with, and it seems maybe permissible to be pretty harsh along this axis in a personal context (though this is to say nothing about what one takes to be the moral worth of another individual — it seems plausible that it is quite impermissible to be in this sense similarly harsh)). Given this, it seems plausible to me that it is morally permissible to, at least in a personal context, engage only with the top $\varepsilon$ fraction of people along certain axes one considers particularly important in the people one hangs out with. It seems likely that there would be characteristics in which especially the tails of the distribution would be populated much more densely by canonical group $X$ than canonical group $Y$. In this (perhaps weak) sense, it seems permissible to be a misogynist, misandrist, racist against any particular set of races, etc. 

I think a reasonable (though trivial) response to this is something like: this has very little to do with what one tries to say is impermissible when one says it is bad to dislike groups of people — even if we talk of one's personal life (e.g. the policy level is at least not directly considered above (though one might be able to say something analogous)), instead, one has in mind a kind of dislike which does not route through anything one might reasonably like or dislike — i.e., an irrational dislike. While this seems reasonable, it is also trivial — well, obviously, one should avoid the irrational kind of X (for any X) — the interestingness is in saying something about the demarcation between rational and irrational kinds of X, which I think I've made essentially no progress on here.

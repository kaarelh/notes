
a small thing i noticed when writing the example (maybe obvious): suppose that one believes that after reflecting on some proposition P for 1000 years (in ideal conditions, etc.), one will come to hold the correct belief on P (P = 'utilitarianism is correct', for example)  
  
this implies that the probability P is true is the same as the probability that one's reflection process leads one to believe P; this lets one treat all weird uncertainties, e.g. moral uncertainty, as uncertainties under reflection — a thing that feels pretty sensible/grounded  
  
(a strong version of this would say that realists and antirealists can agree to just go with the antirealist definition for all object-level purposes (tho i think there might be some subtle issues here))  
  
(this will also work (with some small error bound) if we replace certain belief with just leaning toward P, i.e. that conditional on P being true, one will with 99% probability come to lean toward P, and conditional on P being false, one will with 99% probability come to lean toward not-P)

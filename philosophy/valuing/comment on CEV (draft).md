CEV's view of the future of ethics says it can/should/will be like this: there is some period of figuring ethics out (like, a long reflection), and then a long period of implementing/doing (like, tiling the universe with the best constellation of structures as determined during the earlier coherentification period, or whatever) after that. But maybe future ethical development can/should/will instead be like this: there's just ethical development/thought for a very long time (maybe "forever"/[until the end of life]), contemporaneously with action — like, maybe you're working on your worthwhile projects, and then you [discover new projects and come to the conclusion some are worthwhile] in some complicated way, and you work on those, and so on (with there being no "higher being" in the universe examining/guiding/implementing this process from the outside and considering it good) — it doesn't look like ever having found some ultimate list of worthwhile activities/structures or even some formula for what makes an activity/structure worthwhile; it looks like figuring out what makes sense to do as you go (though of course you can sometimes pause to try to think something through ahead of time, as one often does — it's just that in this case, one shouldn't expect what's understood during a pause of any length to be remotely sufficient for guiding action indefinitely everywhere). Thinking indefinite ethically thoughtful action is what will/should happen is correlated with thinking it is inappropriate to speak of some coherent endpoint of ethics. I say more about this question [here](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/AaGZyhsKYSCN7d8Ki/can-we-naturalize-moral-epistemology?commentId=8vpfnJ8MW67eANAFc) and [here](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/nkeYxjdrWBJvwbnTr/an-advent-of-thought).

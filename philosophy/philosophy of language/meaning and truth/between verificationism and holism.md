
What the heck is it for a sentence to mean anything (for sentences which aim to talk about the actual real world, let's say)? Here are two attempts at answering this question:
* (maybe-straw-)verificationism: Each sentence should correspond to a standalone claim that in such-and-such-a-situation, your visual field would be such-and-such. 
* (maybe-straw-)holism: It doesn't make sense to ask about the meanings of individual sentences or to talk about what individual sentences are doing, only about what a language (or a body of understanding or a theory) says/predicts as a whole.
(These don't perhaps quite address the original question about meaning actually, but let's roll with it anyway.)

Each of the above seems lacking to me. Some obvious issues with verificationism are that (1) it is quite unclear how to describe the circumstances of observation or what's observed except in terms of the very same language, which goes against having our sentences stand alone (or have standalone meanings), (2) even if we tried to ignore this issue by picking some sorta-crisp basic language for describing observational circumstances and observations, we'd still be at a loss regarding which sentence to write down when asked to translate 'there's a tree in front of me' into this language, and (3) to the extent that we can imagine writing down a sorta suitable sentence, it seems like essentially every ordinary sentence would need to be translated to a sentence that is far from being common-sensically equivalent to it. One obvious issue with holism is that surely there's much more fine structure to how language relates to the world than that! Surely something can be said about individual pieces of language having more to do with individual parts of the world, individual situations, individual properties of the world! Another obvious issue with holism is that we are ever-refactoring our language, and it is tremendously helpful that we can look at parts of the language and see them as individually worthy of amending — that we don't have to simply judge all of a language at once, and it is quite helpful for that if we see parts of language as being about parts of the world — an inadequacy in our understanding of a part of the world then immediately suggests a part of our language for revision. A 2.5th (related) issue is that we would like predictions to be produced by the theory cheaply (i.e. with low computational resource requirements, perhaps using only a few concepts, a few subtheories/fragments). Another thing that upsets me about holism is that it is easy to go from it to a picture where entire scientific theories are judged/improved from some external standpoint — e.g., solomonoff induction — whereas really we judge and amend our understanding from 'inside' that understanding — when we discuss which scientific view to adopt/develop, we don't ascend to some separate meta-level — we bring our existing understanding to bear on these matters quite like we bring it to bear on some 'object-level' matter like what probability to assign to it raining tomorrow (and I guess I want to tentatively say that it is good that we do so, though I'm open to proposals to refactoring our understanding which do not have this feature). (And also, again for holism, wtf are predictions like, concretely?) (all this said: maybe sophisticated variants of verificationism and holism can evade these objections?)

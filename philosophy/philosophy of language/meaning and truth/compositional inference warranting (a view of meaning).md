
* intuitively we want to say: phrases (including sentences and words) have meanings, and the meaning of a phrase is determined by the meanings of its constituents
* montague semantics tries to make sense of this via associating to each phrase a certain function involving possible worlds
	* eg the meaning of a sentence will be the function from possible worlds to {true, false} showing which worlds the sentence is true about/of/in
* one could be unhappy with that, wanting something more concrete. like, we could ask something more like: what sort of structure is now in your mind that you have understood a sentence? or maybe: what sort of skills do you now have?
	* there is actually something close to montague semantics here: we have an ability to consider a scene (eg to literally look at a picture) and tell if some sentence is true or false about/of/in it (or unknown from the scene, or not applicable, or sorta confusing). to some extent, this note aims to try to make sense of this skill, but we won't focus on it in particular
	* one could also be happy with montague semantics, but still ask for something else as well, more along the lines of "what sort of structure has been gained". there can be many interesting/useful/correct "theories of meaning", especially if they are theories of different [meaning-like things]/[aspects of meaning] :)
	* we won't be trying to give an account of all the structure in your mind associated to grasping a sentence, just a broad-strokes picture of some of it
* a concrete angle: how come we can "grasp a sentence" and then do some useful (thinking-)stuff?
	* in particular, how come we can draw inferences? 
	* well, there are learned inference rules; there is logic (also? or in other words?). anyway eg there is an (imperfect) inference from "there's a building across the road" to "there's a door within 100 meters"
		* we can note some structure in the set of reasonable inferences — eg "there's a building across the road => there's a door within 100 meters" is similar to "there's a cat across the road => there's another living being within 100 meters"
		* and this isn't merely a surface similarity — there's probably some mental structure associated with "across the road" which is involved in supporting both of these inferences
		* the valid inferences to draw from a sentence are somehow determined by its parts (or more usually, when given a sentence with a lot of additional context, or/ie a collection of sentences)
			* this provides at least a partial answer to the question: why is it useful that a sentence is composed of words? (we could think of this as one way to make precise the answer "it's useful that a sentence is made of words because then understanding the words makes one understand the sentence, so one can understand and think very many diverse thoughts once one has understood relatively very few words")

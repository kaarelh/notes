Here is a thing that makes me sorta dislike both your attempts to precisify deception. I claim that in the intuitive sense of "honest"/"non-deceptive", an honest/non-deceptive person should reasonably often make utterances which (they take to) move their conversation partner further from understanding stuff well. Here is an imo fairly common such pattern:
* Someone is trying to figure out whether a statement $P$ is true. For example, maybe $P=$"We should ban AI.".
* They have identified some claims $Q_i$ which they take to have bearing on $P$. Specifically, let's say that they believe that $\neg Q_1$ would strongly suggest $\neg P$. E.g., continuing with the same example, maybe $Q_1=$"There is a tendency for advanced minds to be more like coherent utility maximizers.".
* Now say you encounter them discussing $Q_1$, and you can guess they are doing this because they take it to have this sort of bearing on $P$.
* Suppose you believe $P$ and also believe $\neg Q_1$.[^1] Suppose that you are participating in their discussion about $Q_1$. Suppose somewhat unrealistically that your only options are to assert $Q_1$ or to assert $\neg Q_1$.[^2] Which would be more honest/non-deceptive?
* I think asserting $\neg Q_1$ is clearly more honest/non-deceptive (in the intuitive sense), and asserting $Q_1$ in an attempt to make the other guy more globally right would be extremely sketchy behavior (even if we suppose you're correct that this assertion makes the other guy more globally right) — like, I would be really concerned if I were talking to a person who acted this way. I'd already be sorta concerned even if they were just running this sort of calculus about my global view when talking to me.

Kinda-generalizing this: it is non-deceptive/honest to always openly present counterevidence to things you believe and/ie to present the best cases against your beliefs when relevant. This causes problems .

this makes me want to suggest something like the following criterion:
* You shouldn't ever assert falsehoods. Also, you should help me understand stuff, but you should emphasize helping me understand the local thing we are considering, without trying to fix me globally. If you think I've hooked up the thing we're locally discussing to other stuff incorrectly, then feel free to try to fix that, but to do that, you should explicitly move the conversation to this other stuff first.

a more vague suggestion:
* More than helping me come to correct beliefs, you are supposed to help me think well. You are supposed to not really have any global things you're trying to do when talking to me — you're just supposed to be locally truth-tracking. You're supposed to be a robustly epistemic loop I can call in my thinking.

But actually I guess maybe your definitions are already fine, at least wrt this class of things, if we tend to take the word "situation" to refer to some local thing.

[^1]: The running example I'm giving is a real example btw, with me being this guy who thinks $P\land (\neg Q_1)$.
[^2]: This literal thing is somewhat unrealistic, but I think it's not too weird to imagine that e.g. discussing $P$ directly would be considered mostly outside the scope of the discussion.

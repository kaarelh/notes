

# the question(s) of interest
i'm pretty sure human thought is [to some meaningful extent]/[in significant part] propositional. it is organized propositionally, maybe? when thinking, we constantly make claims, and we constantly think about claims. why is that? why is it good to think propositionally? what is propositional thinking good for? how come we developed propositional thought? is this a very natural way for a mind to be, or did it just happen that we are this way, but it could happen otherwise in a different mind?

related/same: why do we think of the world in terms of facts? does alphazero think of chess in terms of facts? does a pretrained language model make predictions by thinking in terms of facts? are there other animals that think in terms of facts?

maybe we should ask:
* why does one want to do mathematical thinking propositionally? 
* could we imagine an entity that does mathematical work without thinking propositionally?  

clarification: obviously there is much more to thinking than propositions — thinking in terms of propositions is one good idea/structure of the many "in" a human mind, and that idea/structure alone could not make a human-level mind. the point here is to ask why it is a good idea/structure, whether an analogous idea/structure would show up in other minds, etc.


# how to think about the question

general hypothesis about how to think about this: propositional representation is a type of representation. the question that needs to be answered is why this sort of representation is good. a type of representation is made good by it supporting good thinking (or maybe this is sorta just true by definition). it seems plausible that propositional representations are made good for thinking in some important part by supporting deductive inference? or like idk if we want to say "deductive inference", but maybe just like now one can learn inference rules "on these representations", and this turns out to be really efficient? like, instead of having some direct inference from a picture to an action, and maybe having to learn a shitmess of inference rules for that, one can maybe learn a much smaller system of inference rules on propositions that does better?

# obvious things that the "proposition data structure" is good for in human thought

* supporting knowing facts (this is plausibly tautological, but let me include it anyway)
* supporting deduction
* supporting betting
* supporting action
	* eg i know that store doesn't have milk, so i don't go there to get milk
* supporting thinking about one's beliefs
	* eg being like: i think that i have my keys. but is that really true? let me check
* supporting setting up a model of some stuff. like, i can tell someone who doesn't know how to think of gases that they can think of a gas as a collection of tiny billiard balls bouncing around, and then they might be able to prove the ideal gas law "in" this model
* making analogies
hmm, i guess you could just say it's good for everything humans do? well, maybe it isn't so good for things which are more instinctive/straightforward, but it's good for pretty much any non-obvious thing?
